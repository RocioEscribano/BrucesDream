About Bruce McLaren

Bruce McLaren (1937-1970) was a New Zealand racing driver, automotive designer, engineer, and sports executive. He competed in Formula One from 1958 to 1970, securing four Grand Prix wins and finishing second in the 1960 World Drivers' Championship. McLaren also won the 1966 24 Hours of Le Mans with Ford and established McLaren in 1963, which later achieved nine World Constructors' Championships.

Born in Auckland, McLaren began racing at a young age and entered Formula Two in 1957. By 1959, he joined Cooper’s Formula One team and won his first Grand Prix in 1959 at age 22, a record at the time. He later formed his own racing team, Bruce McLaren Motor Racing, which won its first Grand Prix in 1968.

In endurance racing, McLaren had notable success at the 24 Hours of Le Mans, winning in 1966. He was also a dominant figure in the Can-Am series, achieving unparalleled success in the late 1960s.

As a constructor, McLaren was known for his engineering skills, introducing innovations like the "nostrils" design feature on McLaren cars. His team became known for reliability and success in various racing series, leaving a lasting legacy in motorsports. McLaren tragically died during a test session in 1970, but his name lives on through the McLaren Group, which has achieved numerous racing victories.
